Instructions for Contributors

Your goal is to find a source text in any source language and target language where major AI translation tools (e.g. ChatGPT, Google Translate) produce an inadequate translation, that can be verified with a clear rule.

Source text, target text, and verification rules

The source text can be plain text, an image, a video, or a speech snippet.

  1. Domain or topic: There are no restrictions on what type of text it is or what it talks about. For example, it may be a piece of news, a social media post containing typical slang and informal usage, poetry, something you would see on a signpost, something that would be said in a lecture, the story your grandma told you, the instructions on an annoying government website, your favourite recipe, or anything else. Think broad! Your text can be anything you could imagine being said in some context by someone - for example, the phrase “colourless green ideas”, while meaningless, is still a valid English utterance that someone (like Chomsky) could use, and should be translated correctly. Note that you can write the source text yourself, as well as try something you found in the wild. If you try a text and notice a clear issue, bingo.
  2. Script: If native speakers of your language use more than one script to write it in various contexts (e.g. in official or unofficial contexts), your source text can also use any script, provided that your choice makes sense from a naturalness perspective. For languages without a standardized spelling depending on the script, it’s acceptable to use spelling that you or other native speakers would naturally use, even if that varies between speakers.
  3. Slang and spelling: Use what makes sense for the context of your text! Slang and alternative spelling (“tomorrow” –> “tmrrw”) are acceptable.
  4. Code-switching: If native speakers in your language naturally mix languages in certain contexts (e.g. informal texts), this is acceptable too as long as it is realistic given the context of your source text.

  7. Target text: The target text in the target language is your provided example of one passable translation. Note that there may be many such translations that are rephrasings of one another; you certainly don’t have to list all. Naturally, it should not have the issues you spotted above.
  8. Keep it fair: This is crucial!. A native speaker should be able to understand your text. If the native speaker were a trained translator, they should be able to avoid the issues you found in the machine translation tools.

What counts as a mistranslation?

  1. The mistranslation should be a reasonable deficiency or shift in the meaning, grammar, naturalness, conveyed emotion, formality or register, or another such meaningful characteristic of the produced translation. This deficiency may be subtle, as long as it be noticeable and matter to a native speaker. We list different types of deficiencies below.
  2. Errors due to inherent ambiguity do not count: If your source text has multiple meanings, and you choose one of them as your target text, but the machine translation tool produces another reasonable interpretation, that is not a valid mistake. A good mental check is: would a native speaker also be reasonably confused as to the intended meaning? Is there enough context to determine what the intended meaning is?

What’s a good verification rule?

  1. Rules per text: A particular source text may have up to K verification rules.

  2. Verification rules are targeting tricky things about your source text. Together, your rules should check for what you think and observe could go wrong with translating it. Note that these should certainly cover the issues you spotted with the systems that you try in our interface, but they do not have to be limited to these issues. Feel free to include a rule for something else you believe is tricky, even though the systems you tried happened to get it right.

  3. Format: A verification rule may have the following format:

    • LLM prompt: Write a short instruction for an AI judge, e.g. “Check whether the translation conveys that the speaker feels no inner peace.”

  4. Granularity of your rule: Your verification rule should be specific enough to catch wrong translations but flexible enough to pass any reasonable correct phrasing. Try some correct rephrasings and see if your rule passes them, while still failing various incorrect translations.

Instructions for Reviewers

As a reviewer, your role is to assess whether a submission is a genuine, well-formed example of a difficult but verifiable mistranslation. For each submission, you will see a source text, one or more AI-produced translations, a proposed correct translation, and a verification rule. You should evaluate three things in order.

Is the source text genuinely difficult to translate?

The source text should contain a phrase or expression that is non-trivially hard for AI tools to render correctly. For example, idiomatic expressions, culturally specific references, or poetic language where word-for-word translation misses the meaning.

Reject submissions where the source text is straightforward or where any reasonable translation would capture the meaning correctly.

Is the AI translation actually wrong?

The AI translation should make a meaningful error, not just a stylistic difference, but a shift in meaning that a native speaker would notice. A paraphrase that captures the same sentiment is not sufficient grounds for rejection; the error should be substantive. If a word or a phrase in the original text has multiple translations and only one is considered correct, it should be absolutely clear from the context.

Example: Translating जिया लागे कोनी as “nothing else feels worth living for” rather than “I am not at peace” is a substantive error, the emotional register and meaning are different, even if the poetic effect feels similar.

Is the verification rule correct and well-formed?

The verification rule must:

  • Correctly classify the provided AI translation as wrong and the correct translation as right.
  • Be neither too strict nor too loose: it should accept any reasonable correct translation, not just the exact one provided.
  • Be well-formed: regex patterns should be valid and unambiguous; LLM prompts should be clear and specific enough for a judge to apply consistently.

You may edit the verification rule directly if you can see a straightforward improvement that makes it more robust, without changing its intent.

Decisions

  • Approve: the example is genuine, the mistranslation is substantive, and the verification rule is correct and well-formed.
  • Reject: the source text is too easy, the AI translation is not meaningfully wrong, or the verification rule cannot be made to work.
  • Edit & approve: the example is good, but the verification rule needs a small correction, which you make before approving.
